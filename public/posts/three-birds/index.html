First we found a fledgling in our driveway. At first glance, we couldn’t tell what kind of a bird it was. The feathers had a blueish hue and he was about the same size as a common grackle. We had a freak storm the day before, and the baby bird must have fallen out of the nest prematurely.

There was no cover for him on the driveway, so I gently held him and placed him under my neighbor’s shrubs in the shade. It was then the crows began to crow above me and I realized that this fledgling must have been their baby. It was so small though, and it seemed unlikely that he would survive off the nest.

A few days later, we found a patch of black in our backyard which I assumed was the same fledgling. I felt bad for him, but also didn’t want to touch a bird corpse. I waited for a couple of days, hoping that local scavengers would take care of the carcass. I had no luck, so I donned on my gardening gloves and approached the corpse.

It had begun to decompose and I could see some maggots. I moved as quickly as possible and bagged the crow. It was bigger than the baby I found and it couldn’t have grown that big over a few days. I hoped that the fledgling was alive and well elsewhere.

A couple of days passed before I saw another bird hiding in the shade behind the house. “Not again…” I thought. My wife briefly commented about how we may have bought a house with a crow’s graveyard attached. I watched in apprehension, hoping that the bird would survive, or at least sneak over the fence so that he would die in my neighbor’s backyard.

I had no such luck. By mid morning, he had succumbed to exhaustion and died next to my fence. At least he tried to make it across. With the reluctance of a driver opening a speeding ticket envelope, I approached the carcass. This time, the carcass was fresh, so there were fewer flies.

The downside was that I wasn’t fast enough, so I felt the little body in my grasp while I bagged the little bird. Despite the apparent size, exaggerated by the spread out wings, the body was small, like the fledgling I first found on my driveway. In an odd way, seeing the corpse didn’t trigger this feeling of sadness about death, but touching, feeling the tiny dead thing in my hands set off the on-rush of sad, guilty emotions.

I didn’t think to make a little grave for the bird but hoped that the little fledgling survived and was elsewhere. The head of this bird was too big to be the fledgling.
